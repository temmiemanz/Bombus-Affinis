01  /  Species Overview

The Rusty-Patched
Bumble Bee

Bombus affinis · Cresson, 1863

Once so thick in midwestern meadows it was practically part of the furniture — now one of North America's rarest native bees, surviving in scattered patches of prairie and unmanaged grassland across thirteen states. Its decline didn't happen slowly. Population fell 87% between the late 1990s and the 2010s, making it the first bumble bee ever placed under federal Endangered Species Act protection.

01

Habitat & Niche

Where it lives · Life cycle · Migration patterns

Bombus affinis belongs to open landscapes — tallgrass prairies, unmowed meadows, woodland edges where sunlight reaches the ground long enough for wildflowers to take hold. It nests underground, often inheriting the abandoned burrows of small mammals, and relies on a rotating calendar of bloom: something must be flowering from April through October or a colony cannot store the resources needed to produce new queens before winter.

On migration: B. affinis does not migrate in the conventional sense. However, mated queens perform a seasonal dispersal of their own — emerging from underground hibernation in early spring, sometimes traveling several kilometers to locate a suitable nest site before establishing a new colony entirely alone.
Stage 01  ·  Egg
Egg

Laid singly by the queen inside small wax cells she constructs herself in spring. Each egg is roughly the size of a sesame seed and hatches in approximately four days. At this stage the entire colony is the queen and a few wax thimbles packed with pollen.

Stage 02  ·  Larva
Larva

Pale and grub-like, sealed inside wax cells and fed a pollen-nectar paste by workers. The queen keeps the brood warm by pressing her body against the cell cluster and shivering her flight muscles — generating heat without flying. Larvae molt through four to five instars over roughly two weeks.

Stage 03  ·  Pupa
Pupa

The larva spins a silk cocoon inside its cell. Over ten to fourteen days it undergoes complete metamorphosis — wings, legs, and compound eyes become visible through the translucent cocoon wall. No feeding; no movement. Total internal restructuring.

Stage 04  ·  Adult
Adult

Newly emerged adults spend a day or two inside the nest drying and warming before foraging. Workers remain tied to the colony; queens range broadly in spring and again in late summer. Males leave the nest permanently at maturity — their only biological role is to locate and mate with new queens.

02

Food & Interactions

Diet per life stage · Foraging behavior · Predators

B. affinis is a generalist forager — it visits dozens of wildflower species rather than depending on any single plant. Clovers, goldenrod, wild bergamot, bee balm, and native thistles are all viable sources. This breadth was probably a strength before habitat fragmentation; today it means the bee can survive in patchwork landscapes as long as something is blooming without a gap from spring through fall.

Buzz pollination (sonication): Workers grab a flower and vibrate their flight muscles at a precise frequency, shaking pollen loose from anthers with pores too small for passive collection. Tomatoes, blueberries, and many native wildflowers require this technique. Without bumble bees performing it, those plants produce significantly less fruit and seed.
Egg No autonomous feeding
The egg does not feed independently. It is deposited on a pollen loaf the queen prepares in advance — stored nutrition that surrounds the egg and is available immediately upon hatching.
Larva Passive feeding
Workers regurgitate a concentrated pollen-and-nectar mixture directly into larval cells — high in protein from pollen, high in simple sugars from nectar. Feeding is continuous and proportional to larval growth rate. Nutrition during this stage determines adult body size, which affects foraging efficiency later.
Adult Worker Active foraging
Workers drink nectar on-site for their own flight energy and pack moistened pollen into corbiculae (specialized leg baskets) for transport back to the colony. A single forager may visit hundreds of flowers per trip and carry a pollen load equal to roughly half her own body weight.
Queen Foraging + fat deposition
Spring queens forage to provision the first brood. Late-summer mated queens gorge on nectar before hibernation, building fat reserves large enough to sustain five to six months underground with no food source whatsoever.

Predators & Parasites

Arthropod
Crab Spiders & Ambush Bugs

Sit camouflaged inside flower heads and seize adult foragers mid-visit. Effective enough that experienced foragers sometimes inspect flowers before landing.

Mammal
Skunks, Badgers & Bears

Excavate nests to consume larvae, pupae, and honey stores. Skunks specifically scratch at nest entrances after dark and eat returning workers as they emerge to investigate.

Brood Parasite
Cuckoo Bumble Bees (Psithyrus)

Infiltrate colonies, kill or suppress the host queen, and redirect workers into raising cuckoo offspring. Colony resources are entirely consumed; no further B. affinis reproduction occurs.

Avian / Diptera
Birds & Robber Flies

Intercept adults in flight. Robber flies inject venom rapidly and can take foragers in the air before a sting response is possible.

03

Obstacles to Survival

Natural threats · Human-caused pressures · All life stages

The 87% population collapse over roughly two decades isn't the result of a single event — it's a convergence of pressures hitting simultaneously. Some have been building for over a century; others appeared abruptly in the 1990s.

Primary / Human
Habitat Loss

Less than 4% of original North American tallgrass prairie remains intact. Without continuous bloom across all life stages, colonies cannot store enough resources to produce viable queens before winter.

Primary / Human
Neonicotinoid Pesticides

Systemic insecticides absorbed into plant tissue appear in nectar and pollen at sub-lethal doses. Impairs navigation, memory consolidation, and the ability of foragers to return to the nest — effectively causing colony attrition without direct kill.

Primary / Pathogen
Nosema bombi

A fungal gut pathogen suspected to have devastated North American populations beginning in the 1990s. Likely amplified through commercial bumble bee rearing operations that imported European strains, inadvertently introducing novel pathogen pressure to wild populations with no prior exposure.

Secondary / Climate
Thermal Stress

Bumble bees are cold-adapted and poorly equipped for sustained heat. Their range is compressing northward, but suitable native habitat is not expanding correspondingly — it is simply disappearing from the warmer southern edge.

Secondary / Human
Invasive Plant Species

Non-native plants often bloom at mismatched times or offer nutritionally poor pollen. A gap in mid-season bloom — even two to three weeks — can prevent a colony from storing enough reserves to survive.

Tertiary / Genetic
Inbreeding Depression

Small isolated populations inevitably reproduce among close relatives. In bumble bees, inbreeding triggers sex-determination errors — some larvae that should become workers instead develop as infertile diploid males, reducing colony size and eventual queen output.

04

Adaptations

Traits that enhance survival & reproduction across all life stages

Each trait below evolved across millions of years of selection pressure. Several of them directly conflict with the threats described in Section 03 — which is part of why the population trajectory has been so steep. The bee is well-adapted to its historical environment; it is not adapted to the current one.

Dense Pile Adult — all castes
Branched, feathery body hairs trap a layer of warm air against the exoskeleton. Adults can operate in temperatures near 5°C — far below what most bees can tolerate — allowing queens to emerge earlier in spring and work later into autumn than competing species.
Thoracic Endothermy Adult worker & queen
Flight muscles can be decoupled from the wings and contracted repeatedly — generating heat without producing movement. Queens use this to incubate brood on cold nights; workers use it to warm up before dawn foraging while air temperature is still too low for flight.
Corbiculae Adult workers only
Concave, hair-ringed sections on the hind tibiae function as pollen baskets. Workers moisten collected pollen with nectar and saliva to form a compact pellet that stays mechanically stable during flight. A single worker can carry a load equal to roughly half her body weight back to the colony per trip.
Extended Glossa Adult — all castes
B. affinis has a proportionally long tongue relative to body size, providing access to tubular flowers — clovers, native vetches, wild bergamot — that shorter-tongued bees cannot exploit as efficiently. This expands the effective foraging niche considerably.
Queen Diapause Mated queen — winter
Fertilized queens enter a state of physiological dormancy before winter. Metabolic rate drops dramatically; fat reserves accumulated during late-summer foraging sustain them underground through hard freezes for up to six months with no external food source.
Eusocial Colony Structure All stages
Division of labor — one queen reproduces, workers forage and nurse, males solely mate — means the colony can collectively accomplish what no individual could. A single forager making twenty trips per day contributes to a structure capable of pollinating millions of flowers per season.
Wax Gland Production Adult workers
Workers secrete wax from abdominal glands to construct brood cells, honey pots, and weatherproofing for the nest entrance. The colony builds its entire infrastructure from internally produced materials without relying on anything in the surrounding environment beyond soil and plant matter.
05

Other Interesting Facts

Additional context worth knowing

F1
First bumble bee listed under the U.S. Endangered Species Act. Federal protection was granted in January 2017 after a review confirmed remaining populations in only 13 states — down from a historic range spanning 31 states and two Canadian provinces. The listing occurred on the final day of the Obama administration.
F2
The rusty patch is not present on males. The diagnostic reddish-orange patch on abdominal segment 2 (workers) or segments 2–3 (queens) is absent in males entirely. Males are yellow-faced, without the patch, and are frequently misidentified as a different species without close examination.
F3
A single colony can pollinate up to 20 million flowers per season. Accounting for daily trips across a colony's active months, the cumulative pollination impact is substantial. Localized extinction doesn't just remove a species — it removes a pollination service that native plant populations in that area have depended on for thousands of years.
F4
Queen emergence timing is temperature-dependent, creating climate vulnerability. Queens cue emergence on soil temperature, not calendar date. Warm winters followed by late cold snaps cause premature emergence into a landscape with nothing blooming yet — and a queen that cannot feed in the first two to three weeks after emergence cannot survive to establish a colony.
F5
Citizen science photography is legitimate research data for this species. Much of what is known about surviving B. affinis distribution comes from verified photographs uploaded to the Bumble Bee Watch public database. A backyard photograph in Wisconsin with sufficient detail constitutes a peer-reviewed occurrence record — the kind of contribution that has directly influenced conservation policy.
F6
Colony size is small relative to honeybees, making each queen critical. A full-strength colony peaks at 50–400 workers. A honeybee hive holds 60,000 or more. This means a single predation event — one skunk destroying a nest in May — terminates an entire genetic lineage immediately. There is no redundancy at the colony level.
