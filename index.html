The Rusty-Patched
Bumble Bee

Bombus affinis · Cresson, 1863

The rusty-patched bumble bee was once one of the most common bees in North America and is now one of the rarest. It survives in scattered patches of prairie and unmanaged grassland across thirteen states. Its population fell 87% between the late 1990s and the 2010s, which made it the first bumble bee ever listed under the federal Endangered Species Act.

01

Habitat & Niche

Where it lives · Life cycle · Migration patterns

Bombus affinis needs open, flower-rich landscapes and a continuous bloom from spring through fall to survive. It lives in places like tallgrass prairies, unmowed meadows, and woodland edges where enough sunlight reaches the ground for wildflowers to grow. It nests underground, often in the abandoned burrows of small mammals, and needs a steady rotation of blooming plants from April through October. If there's a gap, the colony can't store enough food to produce new queens before winter.

On migration: B. affinis doesn't migrate the way birds do. But mated queens do disperse on their own each spring, emerging from underground after winter and sometimes traveling several kilometers to find a good nest site before starting a colony from scratch.
Stage 01 · Egg
Egg

Laid one at a time by the queen inside small wax cells she builds herself in spring. Each egg is about the size of a sesame seed and hatches in around four days. At this point the whole colony is just the queen and a few wax thimbles packed with pollen.

Stage 02 · Larva
Larva

Pale and grub-like, sealed inside wax cells and fed a pollen-nectar paste by workers. The queen keeps them warm by pressing her body against the cluster and shivering her flight muscles, which generates heat without actually flying. Larvae molt four to five times over about two weeks.

Stage 03 · Pupa
Pupa

The larva spins a silk cocoon inside its cell. Over ten to fourteen days it goes through complete metamorphosis. Wings, legs, and compound eyes become visible through the cocoon wall. No feeding, no movement. Total rebuild from the inside out.

Stage 04 · Adult
Adult

New adults spend a day or two in the nest drying out and warming up before they start foraging. Workers stay tied to the colony. Queens range far in spring and again in late summer. Males leave the nest for good at maturity since their only job is to find and mate with new queens.

02

Food & Interactions

Diet per life stage · Foraging behavior · Predators

B. affinis feeds from dozens of different wildflower species, which makes it more flexible than bees that depend on just one plant. Clovers, goldenrod, wild bergamot, bee balm, and native thistles are all fair game. That flexibility was probably an advantage before habitat fragmentation. Today it means the bee can get by in patchy landscapes as long as something is blooming continuously from spring through fall.

Buzz pollination (sonication): Workers grab onto a flower and vibrate their flight muscles at a specific frequency, shaking pollen loose from anthers that have openings too small for passive collection. Tomatoes, blueberries, and a lot of native wildflowers need this. Without bumble bees doing it, those plants produce noticeably less fruit and seed.
EggNo autonomous feeding
The egg doesn't feed on its own. The queen deposits it on a pollen loaf she prepared in advance, so the food is right there when it hatches.
LarvaPassive feeding
Workers regurgitate a pollen-and-nectar mix directly into the larval cells. Feeding happens constantly and keeps up with how fast the larva is growing. How well it eats at this stage determines its adult body size, which affects how efficiently it forages later.
Adult WorkerActive foraging
Workers drink nectar on-site for their own flight energy and pack moistened pollen into corbiculae, which are specialized baskets on their hind legs, to carry back to the colony. A single forager might visit hundreds of flowers per trip and carry a pollen load close to half her own body weight.
QueenForaging + fat storage
Spring queens forage to feed the first batch of larvae. Mated queens in late summer eat as much as possible before hibernation, building up fat reserves that have to last them five or six months underground with no food at all.

Predators & Parasites

Arthropod
Crab Spiders & Ambush Bugs

Hide inside flower heads and grab foragers mid-visit. They're good enough at it that experienced bees will sometimes pause and inspect a flower before landing.

Mammal
Skunks, Badgers & Bears

Dig up nests to get at the larvae, pupae, and honey. Skunks have a specific trick where they scratch at the entrance at night and eat workers as they come out to check.

Brood Parasite
Cuckoo Bumble Bees (Psithyrus)

Move into the colony, kill or suppress the host queen, and get the workers to raise their offspring instead. Once that happens the colony's resources go entirely to the cuckoo bee's young, and no new B. affinis are produced.

Avian / Diptera
Birds & Robber Flies

Catch adults mid-flight. Robber flies are fast enough to take a forager out of the air before she can sting.

03

Obstacles to Survival

Natural threats · Human-caused pressures · All life stages

The 87% population collapse wasn't caused by one thing — it's the result of several major threats hitting at the same time. Some had been building for over a century, and others showed up suddenly in the 1990s.

Primary / Human
Habitat Loss

Less than 4% of the original North American tallgrass prairie is still intact. Without something blooming throughout the whole season, colonies can't store enough food to produce new queens before winter.

Primary / Human
Neonicotinoid Pesticides

These insecticides get absorbed into plant tissue and show up in nectar and pollen at low doses. They don't kill bees outright but mess with navigation and memory, making it harder for foragers to find their way back to the nest. The colony slowly loses workers.

Primary / Pathogen
Nosema bombi

A fungal gut pathogen that's thought to have hammered North American populations starting in the 1990s. It was probably made worse by commercial bumble bee breeding operations, which exposed wild populations to pathogens they'd never encountered.

Secondary / Climate
Thermal Stress

Bumble bees are built for cold and handle heat badly. Their range is shifting northward, but suitable habitat isn't expanding to meet it. It's just disappearing from the warmer southern end.

Secondary / Human
Invasive Plant Species

Non-native plants often bloom at the wrong times or provide low-quality pollen. Even a two or three week gap in mid-season bloom can be enough to keep a colony from building up enough reserves to survive.

Tertiary / Genetic
Inbreeding Depression

When populations get small, bees end up breeding with close relatives. In bumble bees this causes sex-determination errors that produce sterile diploid males instead of workers, so colonies get smaller and eventually fail to produce queens.

04

Adaptations

Traits that enhance survival & reproduction across all life stages

This bee has a set of remarkable physical and behavioral traits built for survival, but most of them were shaped by a world that no longer exists. Each of these traits developed over millions of years of natural selection. Several of them directly clash with the threats in Section 03, which is a big part of why the decline has been so fast. The bee is well-suited to the world it evolved in. It's not suited to the world it's in now.

Dense PileAdult, all castes
The branched, feathery body hairs trap warm air against the exoskeleton. Adults can function in temperatures near 5 degrees C, which is much colder than most bees can handle. This lets queens emerge earlier in spring and keep working later into fall.
Thoracic EndothermyAdult worker and queen
Flight muscles can be disconnected from the wings and contracted on their own, which generates heat without actually moving the wings. Queens use this to keep the brood warm at night. Workers use it to warm themselves up before flying when it's still too cold at dawn.
CorbiculaeAdult workers only
Concave, hair-ringed areas on the hind legs work as pollen baskets. Workers mix collected pollen with nectar and saliva to form a pellet that holds together during flight. A single worker can carry close to half her own body weight in pollen per trip.
Extended GlossaAdult, all castes
B. affinis has a longer tongue relative to its body size than many other bees, which gives it access to tubular flowers like clovers, native vetches, and wild bergamot that shorter-tongued bees can't reach as easily. That opens up a wider range of food sources.
Queen DiapauseMated queen, winter
Mated queens go dormant before winter. Their metabolism slows way down, and the fat they built up through late-summer foraging keeps them alive underground through freezing temperatures for up to six months with no food.
Eusocial StructureAll stages
The colony divides up the work: the queen reproduces, workers forage and care for larvae, and males exist to mate. This lets the colony do collectively what no single bee could. One forager making twenty trips a day is part of a system that pollinates millions of flowers in a season.
Wax Gland ProductionAdult workers
Workers produce wax from glands on their abdomen and use it to build brood cells, honey pots, and weatherproofing at the nest entrance. The whole structure is built from materials the colony makes internally. All they need from the outside world is soil to nest in.
05

Other Interesting Facts

Additional context worth knowing

F1
First bumble bee listed under the U.S. Endangered Species Act. Federal protection was granted in January 2017 after a review found populations in only 13 states, down from a historic range that covered 31 states and two Canadian provinces. The listing happened on the last day of the Obama administration.
F2
Males don't have the rusty patch. The reddish-orange patch on the abdomen shows up on workers and queens but is completely absent on males. Males are yellow-faced and get misidentified as a different species pretty often without a close look.
F3
A single colony can pollinate up to 20 million flowers in one season. When you add up all the daily foraging trips over an active season, the number gets massive. When a local population disappears, it doesn't just remove a species. It removes a pollination service that the local plants have relied on for thousands of years.
F4
Queens emerge based on soil temperature, not the calendar. That makes them vulnerable to climate swings. A warm winter followed by a late cold snap can push queens out of hibernation too early, before anything is blooming. A queen that can't find food in the first two or three weeks after emerging won't survive to start a colony.
F5
Photos taken by regular people count as real scientific data for this species. A lot of what's known about where B. affinis still survives comes from verified photos uploaded to the Bumble Bee Watch database. A clear backyard photo can become a peer-reviewed occurrence record and has directly affected conservation decisions.
F6
These colonies are tiny compared to honeybees, so every queen matters. A full-strength colony tops out at 50 to 400 workers. A honeybee hive can have 60,000 or more. That means one predation event, like a skunk raiding a nest in May, wipes out an entire genetic lineage on the spot. There's no backup.
